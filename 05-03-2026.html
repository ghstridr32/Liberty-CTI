This issue should be read as a leadership warning, not a technical recap. This issue of The Alamo Threat Brief covers the week of 26 April 2026 and assesses a threat environment defined by four simultaneous shifts that Texas organizations cannot afford to treat as separate problems. North Korea has weaponized AI-assisted software development tools to poison developer pipelines — a direct threat to Texas defense, AI, and fintech employers who run software development operations. Russia has escalated from prior warnings to an active advisory posture against U.S. energy logistics and maritime infrastructure, with Gulf Coast LNG terminals and Texas ports explicitly in scope. Pro-Iranian hacktivist groups are increasing low-sophistication OT attacks against the exact infrastructure profile Texas concentrates — water, energy, and municipal systems. And actively exploited vulnerabilities this week expose enterprise server management and Windows environments at a speed that widens the gap between disclosure and organizational response. The central analytic judgment: the convergence of DPRK developer-targeting, Russian maritime pressure, Iranian OT escalation, and accelerating exploit tempo means Texas organizations can no longer sequence their risk — they must manage it in parallel.

Decision 1 — Developer Pipeline Integrity

If your organization employs software developers, runs AI agent workflows, or operates fintech or crypto-adjacent infrastructure, decide this week whether your software dependency controls — npm audits, package provenance checks, CI/CD integrity — are mature enough to detect a state-sponsored malicious package before it executes inside your environment. DPRK is now using AI tools to generate malware. The detection gap is real.

Decision 2 — Maritime and Energy Logistics Exposure

If your organization operates, depends on, or insures Gulf Coast ports, LNG export terminals, pipelines, or maritime logistics, decide whether your cyber resilience posture for those operations has been validated against the Russian targeting profile described in the DOE/MARAD advisory this week. Insurance, contractual SLAs, and continuity planning must now account for a named advisory — not a theoretical scenario.

Decision 3 — OT and Municipal Exposure

If your organization operates water, energy, or municipal infrastructure with any internet-facing OT components, decide whether external attack surface management for those systems has been validated in the last 30 days. Iranian hacktivist groups are exploiting low-hanging OT exposure — not sophisticated zero-days. The risk is operational disruption and public concern, not just a technical incident.

DPRK Has Crossed Into AI-Enabled Supply Chain Warfare Against Developers

The analytically significant development this week is not that North Korea targeted developers again. It is how. The PromptMink campaign, attributed to DPRK-nexus actor Famous Chollima by ReversingLabs, introduced a malicious npm package through a commit to an autonomous trading agent — a commit co-authored by an AI large language model. The malware itself shows signs of AI-assisted generation. This is not a rhetorical point about AI being misused. It is a concrete operational shift: DPRK has begun embedding AI tools into the production of malicious software supply chain attacks, reducing the human expertise required and increasing the plausibility and scale of what gets introduced into developer environments.

For Texas, this matters on two levels. First, the Contagious Interview campaign — the broader DPRK ecosystem this activity sits inside — has been targeting software developers, AI engineers, and fintech workers for years through fake job offers, technical interview traps, and poisoned repositories. Texas defense contractors, AI development firms, and financial technology companies are all inside that target profile. Second, the supply chain vector is particularly dangerous because it exploits the trust developers place in package registries. A developer running a standard npm install inside a defense contractor's CI/CD pipeline is not doing something careless. They are being targeted through a system they are supposed to trust.

The DPRK Axios npm compromise from late March — in which STARDUST CHOLLIMA used stolen maintainer credentials to push malware into a package downloaded more than 100,000 times per week — underscores that this is a sustained campaign, not a one-time event. The operational tempo has surged since Q4 2025 and shows no sign of slowing. Bottom line: any Texas organization running software development operations should treat npm and PyPI dependency integrity as a board-level supply chain risk, not a developer hygiene item.

Section II

Fused Analysis: What Changed This Week

DPRK — AI-Assisted Malware Enters the Developer Supply Chain

ReversingLabs this week published analysis of PromptMink, a malicious npm campaign attributed to DPRK-nexus actor Famous Chollima, part of the broader Contagious Interview ecosystem. The analytically significant element is not just the campaign — Famous Chollima has been poisoning developer repositories for years. It is that the malicious package was introduced through a commit co-authored by an AI large language model, and the malware itself shows signs of AI-assisted code generation. The campaign deploys stealers targeting cryptocurrency wallets and developer credentials via multi-layer dependency architectures designed to survive takedown — when second-layer malicious packages are removed, they are swiftly replaced.

This sits within a broader DPRK software supply chain acceleration. STARDUST CHOLLIMA compromised the Axios npm package in late March using stolen maintainer credentials, deploying upgraded ZshBucket malware across Linux, macOS, and Windows to a package downloaded more than 100,000 times per week. DPRK's operational tempo across npm and PyPI has surged since Q4 2025 and has not slowed.

Liberty CTI assesses that any Texas organization running software development operations — particularly in defense, AI, or fintech — should treat the DPRK developer supply chain campaign as an active threat to their CI/CD environment, not a theoretical risk. The window between package publication and organizational exposure is now measured in hours.

Russia — DOE/MARAD Advisory Names Energy Logistics as Active Target

A joint advisory from the U.S. Department of Energy and the Maritime Administration this week warned of increased Russian cyber targeting of energy logistics and maritime infrastructure, explicitly naming LNG terminals, ports, pipelines, maritime transport, and refineries as sectors in scope. This is not a standing background warning — it is an advisory issued against an active and assessed threat pattern. For Texas, the advisory lands directly on the state's most strategically significant export infrastructure: Gulf Coast LNG export terminals are central to U.S. energy commitments to European and Asian allies, and Texas ports handle a material share of U.S. energy commodity flows.

// Liberty CTI Assessment

A successful Russian cyber operation against Texas LNG or port infrastructure would not be a contained incident. It would affect export commitments, commodity pricing, insurance underwriting, and allied energy security simultaneously — at a moment when Russia has direct incentive to demonstrate that U.S. energy export capacity is fragile.

Iran — Hacktivist Pressure Moves From Background to Active Advisory

DHS and CISA issued situational reporting this week on increased pro-Iranian hacktivist group targeting of U.S. infrastructure websites and low-level OT environments. The reporting covers energy, water, municipal systems, healthcare, and small OT environments — precisely the profile of infrastructure that Texas concentrates in high density outside major metropolitan areas. The capability level involved is generally low-sophistication: website defacements, DDoS, and exploitation of default credentials or internet-exposed interfaces. But in OT environments, low-sophistication attacks can produce operational disruption, public safety concern, and significant reputational damage even without advanced intrusion capability.

Active Exploitation — cPanel and Windows Shell Flaws Under Attack Now

CISA added CVE-2026-41940 — a missing-authentication vulnerability in WebPros cPanel and WHM — to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog after hosting providers confirmed active, ongoing attacks. cPanel is among the most widely deployed web hosting management platforms globally; an authentication bypass allowing administrative access is a high-value exploitation target for credential theft, lateral movement, and persistent access. Separately, Microsoft revised its advisory for CVE-2026-32202, a Windows Shell spoofing vulnerability previously used by APT28 (Fancy Bear) against Ukraine and EU targets, to acknowledge active exploitation in the wild. CISA also cataloged two additional vulnerabilities including a ConnectWise ScreenConnect path traversal flaw — a platform used heavily by managed service providers and IT support operations throughout Texas.

Central Texas — Data-Center Buildout Creates Compounding Local Dependencies

Austin American-Statesman reporting this week details the scale of Central Texas data-center construction and the compounding pressures it is creating across local power, water, construction labor, and public-health infrastructure. This item is not primarily a cyber story — but it is an infrastructure dependency story with direct cyber risk implications. The faster data centers concentrate in a geographic area, the more that area's municipal services, grid capacity, and emergency response systems become load-bearing dependencies for organizations those data centers serve. A cyber disruption targeting any one of those dependencies — grid, water supply, cooling infrastructure — in a high-concentration environment creates compounding effects that isolated facility analysis does not capture.

Section III

Geopolitical Drivers and Texas Cyber Implications

DPRK — Sanctions Pressure Is Driving Operational Innovation, Not Retreat

Why It Matters: DOJ enforcement and Treasury sanctions in recent weeks have not slowed DPRK's developer-targeting campaigns. They have accelerated them. North Korea's WMD program finance requirements are structural — enforcement actions raise operational costs but do not change the underlying requirement to generate hard currency through cyber operations. The introduction of AI-assisted malware generation represents DPRK adapting to that pressure by reducing the human expertise required to produce and sustain campaigns.

Business Risk (Texas Lens): Texas defense contractors, AI development firms, and fintech employers who rely on remote or contract technical workers, use npm or PyPI in their development pipelines, or operate crypto-adjacent financial products are inside the target profile that DPRK has consistently pursued.

// Executive Takeaway

DPRK is not slowing down. It is automating. The advisory-to-exploit window in developer supply chains is now hours, not weeks. Organizations that treat dependency security as a quarterly review item are miscalibrated for this environment.

Russia — Energy Warfare Logic Makes Texas a Named Priority

Why It Matters: Russia has a direct strategic incentive to demonstrate that U.S. LNG export capacity is vulnerable. American energy exports to Europe and Asia have materially undermined Moscow's energy coercion leverage — the single most powerful economic weapon Russia deployed in 2022. Targeting Gulf Coast export infrastructure is not opportunistic. It is consistent with a deliberate campaign to reassert energy leverage and impose costs on U.S. energy infrastructure that supports allied energy security.

Business Risk (Texas Lens): Port of Houston, Sabine Pass LNG, Freeport LNG, and Corpus Christi LNG terminals are collectively among the most strategically significant energy export nodes in the world. Operators, logistics providers, insurers, and contractors serving those facilities are all inside the threat perimeter described by the DOE/MARAD advisory.

// Executive Takeaway

The DOE/MARAD advisory is not background noise. It is a named warning against a named sector. Texas LNG and port operators should treat it as a board-level risk disclosure event, not a technical team notification.

Iran — Low-Capability Actors Can Still Create High-Impact Disruption

Why It Matters: Pro-Iranian hacktivist groups operate with limited technical sophistication compared to IRGC-linked APT actors. But OT disruption does not require sophisticated capability when the target environment has internet-exposed control systems with default credentials, no segmentation, and no monitoring. Texas has a large footprint of exactly those environments across smaller municipal water and energy operators outside major metros.

Business Risk (Texas Lens): A visible OT disruption at a Texas water or power facility — even one producing no long-term damage — generates public concern, regulatory attention, and executive accountability pressure that can exceed the operational impact of the incident itself.

// Executive Takeaway

Iranian hacktivist groups are not targeting the most hardened environments. They are targeting the most exposed ones. For Texas municipal operators, the question is not whether they are a strategic priority — it is whether they are a soft target of opportunity.

Section VII

Executive Implications and Recommended Actions

1 — Audit Software Dependencies Before the Next Deployment

Require software teams to run dependency audits against current npm and PyPI lock files, flag any package with a recent publish date, shallow download history, or AI-generation indicators, and implement a software composition analysis tool in CI/CD pipelines if one is not already active. What happens if you get this wrong: a malicious package executes inside a defense or fintech development environment and you discover the breach through a DOJ notification, not your own monitoring.

2 — Brief Gulf Coast Operations Leadership on the DOE/MARAD Advisory

If your organization operates, insures, or provides logistics services to LNG terminals, Texas ports, or pipeline infrastructure, ensure that operations leadership — not only IT security — has read the DOE/MARAD advisory and has validated cyber resilience of port operations software, vessel scheduling systems, and third-party maritime service providers. What happens if you get this wrong: a disruption event occurs and your organization's board discovers the advisory existed and was treated as a technical notification rather than an operational risk.

3 — Patch cPanel and Windows Shell Flaws This Week, Not Next Sprint

CVE-2026-41940 and CVE-2026-32202 are both on the CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities list with active exploitation confirmed. CISA mandates federal agency remediation within defined windows — private sector organizations should treat KEV listings as equivalent urgency signals. What happens if you get this wrong: a threat actor gains administrative access to your web hosting infrastructure or Windows environment through a flaw that was publicly disclosed, listed as exploited, and available for patching before the compromise occurred.

4 — Validate OT External Attack Surface for Water and Energy Operators

Municipal water authorities, energy co-ops, and industrial operators in Texas should confirm — not assume — that no PLC, HMI, or SCADA interface is internet-reachable without authentication, segmentation, and active monitoring. Use an external attack surface management scan if one has not been run in the last 30 days. What happens if you get this wrong: a pro-Iranian hacktivist group finds and exploits a default-credential interface, generates public concern about infrastructure reliability, and creates regulatory scrutiny — from an attack that required no advanced capability to execute.

5 — Map Data-Center Dependencies Into Continuity Plans

For any Texas organization whose critical operations depend on Central Texas data centers, cloud providers, or colocation facilities, confirm that continuity plans account for scenarios where data-center operations are constrained by grid, water, or labor disruptions — not only cyber incidents. What happens if you get this wrong: your continuity plan assumes the data center keeps running during a regional infrastructure stress event when the real dependency chain has already been disrupted upstream.

Section VIII

Analyst's Note

The thread connecting this week's intelligence is speed. DPRK is using AI to accelerate malware production — collapsing the time between capability development and deployment into developer pipelines. Active exploitation of cPanel and Windows Shell vulnerabilities is being confirmed within days of disclosure, not weeks. Pro-Iranian hacktivist groups are moving quickly against low-hanging OT targets because the barrier to entry is minimal. And Russia is operating against maritime logistics infrastructure at a tempo that warranted a formal advisory, not just a standing background warning.

The organizational response to speed cannot be slower deliberation. It has to be faster decision-making with better pre-positioned defaults. That means software teams have standing authority and tooling to reject unvalidated packages without waiting for a security review cycle. It means operational leaders at LNG and port facilities have already read the advisory and have a validated action list, not a pending briefing. It means OT operators have already confirmed their external attack surface rather than relying on the assumption that prior hardening is still current.

Texas organizations that are still treating cybersecurity as a quarterly reporting function are running a different clock than the adversary. The practical question for this week is not whether your security team knows about these developments. It is whether the executives who own the business decisions those developments require have the information and the authority to act before the next incident forces the issue.
