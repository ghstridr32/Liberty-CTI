This issue of the Alamo Threat Brief covers April 7–13, 2026. The week's dominant development is a joint federal advisory from six U.S. agencies confirming that Iranian-affiliated APT actors have actively disrupted programmable logic controllers and SCADA systems at U.S. water, energy, and government facilities since at least March 2026. This is not a warning about potential future risk. It is a confirmation of ongoing harm. A critical FortiClient EMS zero-day under active exploitation, a $285 million North Korean social engineering operation, and China-linked Medusa ransomware deployment round out a week that signals simultaneous escalation across multiple adversary tracks.

Iran Escalates to OT Disruption: A Confirmed New Threshold in Critical Infrastructure Attack

The joint advisory issued Tuesday by the FBI, CISA, NSA, EPA, Department of Energy, and U.S. Cyber Command is analytically significant not because Iranian actors targeted U.S. infrastructure — that has been assessed as likely for months — but because it confirms they succeeded. Victims experienced operational disruption and financial loss. PLCs were manipulated. SCADA displays showed falsified data. Industrial processes were disrupted.

The targeted devices are Rockwell Automation and Allen-Bradley PLCs — the dominant industrial controllers in U.S. energy production, water treatment, petrochemical processing, and manufacturing. Texas operates the largest independent power grid in the United States and hosts the highest concentration of Gulf Coast LNG terminals, Permian Basin production infrastructure, and refinery capacity in the country. The density of Rockwell/Allen-Bradley deployments in Texas is among the highest nationally.

The advisory also arrives in the same week that a FortiClient EMS zero-day is being actively exploited against enterprise endpoint management infrastructure, Storm-1175 is deploying Medusa ransomware across healthcare and finance, and North Korea has confirmed its most sophisticated financial theft operation of the year. The convergence of simultaneous, confirmed adversary activity across all four major state-actor categories in a single week is the defining analytical signal of Q2 2026. Texas critical infrastructure organizations are operating in a threat environment where multiple capable adversaries are active simultaneously — not sequentially.

Section II

What Happened This Week

Iran — Confirmed OT Disruption at U.S. Energy and Water Facilities

Six U.S. federal agencies issued a joint advisory on April 7 warning that Iranian-affiliated APT actors have exploited internet-facing OT devices — specifically Rockwell Automation/Allen-Bradley PLCs — to cause disruptions across U.S. critical infrastructure since at least March 2026. The advisory explicitly states that victims experienced operational disruption and financial loss, and that the attacks involved malicious interaction with PLC project files and manipulation of data displayed on HMI and SCADA displays. Sectors confirmed as affected: energy, water and wastewater systems, and government facilities. The campaign echoes the 2023 CyberAv3ngers PLC operations but is broader, faster, and targeting systems with a higher consequence of failure.

More than 3,000 Rockwell Automation/Allen-Bradley devices remain visible on the public internet. The advisory's recommended immediate actions: disconnect PLCs from public internet access; set physical mode switches to "run" position to prevent remote modification; enable MFA on all remote access paths; audit logs for suspicious activity since March 2026; create and test backups of PLC logic and configurations. These are not aspirational security goals — they are the minimum required response to confirmed active exploitation.

FortiClient EMS — Double Zero-Day, CISA Emergency Deadline

Fortinet issued an emergency advisory on April 4 for CVE-2026-35616, an improper access control vulnerability in FortiClient Enterprise Management Server (CVSS 9.1) that allows unauthenticated attackers to execute arbitrary code via crafted requests. CISA added it to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on April 6, mandating federal remediation by April 9 — a three-day window reflecting CISA's assessment of severity. Exploitation was first observed by watchTowr honeypots on March 31, before the patch existed. This is the second critical FortiClient EMS flaw to be actively exploited in weeks; CVE-2026-21643 (also CVSS 9.1) remains under active exploitation alongside the new zero-day. More than 2,000 FortiClient EMS instances remain internet-exposed and unpatched.

// Liberty CTI Assessment

Two critical zero-days in the same product within weeks of each other signals sustained adversary focus on a specific, high-value target — enterprise endpoint management infrastructure. FortiClient EMS controls device policy, manages VPN access, and governs application compliance across corporate endpoints. A compromised EMS server gives an attacker the ability to push malicious policies across an entire managed fleet. Organizations running FortiClient EMS and exposed to the internet should treat this as an emergency response situation, not a scheduled patch cycle.

DPRK — $285M Drift Hack: Six Months, Twelve Minutes

Drift Protocol confirmed on April 5–7 that its April 1 breach — which drained $285 million in twelve minutes — was the culmination of a six-month social engineering operation by UNC4736 (AppleJeus/Citrine Sleet), a DPRK state-sponsored hacking group. Attackers posed as a quantitative trading firm, attended major crypto conferences internationally starting in fall 2025, built sustained in-person relationships with Drift contributors, and ultimately socially engineered multisig signers into pre-approving hidden governance transactions. The attack itself combined a manufactured fictitious token (CarbonVote), oracle manipulation, and pre-signed durable nonce transactions to drain $285 million in 31 withdrawals. The in-person operatives were not North Korean nationals — DPRK deployed third-party intermediaries for face-to-face relationship-building. TRM Labs and Elliptic attributed the attack to DPRK with medium-to-high confidence. This is the eighteenth DPRK crypto operation of 2026; the regime has stolen over $300 million year-to-date.

China / Storm-1175 — Medusa Ransomware via Zero-Day Chains

Microsoft Threat Intelligence confirmed that Storm-1175, a China-based threat actor, is conducting "high-velocity" attacks using combinations of zero-day and N-day vulnerabilities to deploy Medusa ransomware. Confirmed target sectors include healthcare, education, professional services, and finance in the U.S., UK, and Australia. Storm-1175 has leveraged zero-day exploits in some cases before public disclosure, and has chained multiple exploits for post-compromise lateral movement. This represents a behavioral departure from traditional PRC cyber operations, which have historically emphasized espionage pre-positioning over financial disruption.

Section III

Texas Sector Implications

⚡ Energy
CRITICAL — ACTIVE ADVISORY
What happened: Joint federal advisory confirms Iranian-affiliated actors have disrupted PLCs and SCADA systems in energy sector facilities since March 2026, targeting Rockwell Automation/Allen-Bradley devices.

Why it matters in Texas: Texas operates ERCOT — the largest independent power grid in the U.S. — and hosts the highest concentration of Gulf Coast LNG export terminals, Permian Basin production operations, and refinery infrastructure in the country. CPS Energy's JBSA contracts create a direct national security dependency on Texas grid reliability. The density of Rockwell/Allen-Bradley PLC deployments in Texas is among the highest nationally.

Bottom-line risk: Any Texas energy or utility organization with internet-exposed PLCs, HMI systems, or SCADA displays should treat the federal advisory as a direct and immediate warning. Mitigation is specific: disconnect OT devices from the public internet, audit access logs since March 2026, set Rockwell devices to "run" mode. This is not a future risk mitigation exercise — it is incident response preparation for a threat that has already caused harm.
💰 Finance
HIGH — DPRK METHODOLOGY
What happened: DPRK UNC4736 completed a six-month in-person social engineering operation that drained $285M from Drift Protocol. Separately, Storm-1175 is targeting finance sector organizations with Medusa ransomware via zero-day exploit chains.

Why it matters in Texas: USAA, Frost Bank, and the broader DFW financial sector represent high-value DPRK targets — particularly organizations with digital asset custody, fintech partnerships, or blockchain-adjacent infrastructure. The social engineering methodology — conference attendance, sustained relationship-building, fabricated professional credentials — is directly applicable to private equity and banking relationship models common in Texas.

Bottom-line risk: Texas financial institutions should review vendor onboarding processes, third-party integration approvals, and any external parties with system access sourced from conference or networking relationships. Specifically: any external party that requested administrative access, system integration rights, or developer-level credentials following a conference meeting in the past twelve months warrants review.
♥ Healthcare
HIGH — STORM-1175 + FORTICLIENT
What happened: Storm-1175 explicitly targeted healthcare organizations with Medusa ransomware this week using chained zero-day exploits. FortiClient EMS exploitation — which enables management-layer compromise — is acutely dangerous for hospital systems with centrally managed endpoint security.

Why it matters in Texas: Texas Medical Center — the world's largest medical complex — and regional hospital networks share the structural vulnerabilities that define the Storm-1175 target profile: sensitive data, operational pressure that delays patching, and FortiClient deployments that represent an attractive management-plane attack surface.

Bottom-line risk: Healthcare CISOs should treat CVE-2026-35616 as an emergency — not a scheduled maintenance item. If FortiClient EMS is deployed and internet-exposed, assume it is targeted. Apply the hotfix immediately and review all endpoint policy changes pushed since March 31, 2026.
🛡 Defense / DIB
HIGH — FORTICLIENT + IRAN OT
What happened: FortiClient EMS is widely deployed across defense contractors for endpoint compliance and VPN management. The JBSA contractor ecosystem uses Fortinet products extensively. Iranian OT targeting, while confirmed against water and energy, follows a historical playbook that expands to defense-adjacent infrastructure.

Why it matters in Texas: A compromised FortiClient EMS server inside a defense contractor's environment gives an adversary the ability to alter endpoint compliance posture, manipulate VPN configurations, and potentially access cleared personnel networks. This is a supply chain risk to defense programs, not a generic IT risk.

Bottom-line risk: All Texas DIB organizations running FortiClient EMS should apply the CVE-2026-35616 hotfix immediately and audit EMS-pushed policy changes since March 31. Both CVE-2026-35616 and CVE-2026-21643 should be treated as active exploitation events requiring incident response validation, not routine patching.
⬡ AI / Data Centers
ELEVATED — FLOWISE + LITELLM
What happened: Flowise, a widely deployed open-source AI platform, has a maximum-severity (CVSS 10.0) flaw under active exploitation this week. The LiteLLM/Trivy supply-chain extortion wave from ATB-2026-14 continues. The DPRK operation confirms developer workstations remain priority targets for state-sponsored credential harvesting.

Why it matters in Texas: Texas leads the nation in data center interconnection requests to ERCOT — 226 GW of pending load. The combination of rapid AI infrastructure expansion and active developer-facing supply chain attacks creates a compounding exposure window. Organizations deploying both Flowise and LiteLLM are facing maximum-severity vulnerabilities in both platforms simultaneously.

Bottom-line risk: Audit Flowise deployments and apply patches immediately. Complete LiteLLM/Trivy dependency review from ATB-2026-14 if not already done — extortion contacts from the supply-chain cascade are expected to peak between mid-April and mid-May.

Section VI

Analyst's Note

This week's most significant analytical development is not a single incident — it is a convergence. The joint federal advisory on Iranian OT disruption, the FortiClient zero-day exploitation, the DPRK Drift operation, and Storm-1175's ransomware campaign all arrived in the same week. Each is significant individually. Together, they illustrate the defining challenge for Texas critical infrastructure in Q2 2026: the threat environment is not sequential. Multiple capable adversaries are operating simultaneously, targeting different access vectors, with different objectives and different tolerances for risk.

Iran is targeting the physical layer — PLCs, HMIs, SCADA displays — with confirmed disruptive intent. China-linked actors are deploying ransomware through zero-day chains against the sectors Texas cannot afford to lose. North Korea is draining financial reserves through social engineering operations that bypass technical controls entirely. And the criminal ecosystem continues exploiting the vulnerabilities all three state actors help identify.

The FortiClient story deserves particular attention as a structural warning. Two critical zero-days in the same product within weeks of each other is evidence of sustained adversary focus on a specific attack surface. Enterprise endpoint management systems are attractive because they provide scale: compromise one EMS server, and you may control thousands of endpoints. That leverage is why defenders use them — and it is exactly why attackers target them.

For Texas executives, the operative question this week is not "are we likely targeted?" It is: what would we see if we were already inside, and when would we see it? The Iranian advisory answers this question for OT environments with precision: manipulated SCADA displays, modified project files, disrupted PLCs. That is what detection looks like. Organizations that have not audited their OT anomaly detection baseline are not positioned to answer the question before an incident answers it for them.
