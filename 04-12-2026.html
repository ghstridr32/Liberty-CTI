This issue of the Alamo Threat Brief aligns to the week of 13 April 2026 and assesses a threat environment shaped by simultaneous pressure from Iranian OT disruption, PRC pre-positioning, DPRK financial theft, and industrialized ransomware targeting—each tied to larger geopolitical dynamics that are increasing cyber risk to Texas organizations. The central analytic judgment is that Texas organizations are no longer managing isolated cyber risk. They are operating inside an interconnected battlespace where disruption in energy, healthcare, finance, and the defense industrial base can cascade quickly across the state.

Decision 1 — OT Exposure

If you operate energy, water, industrial, or petrochemical infrastructure, decide this week whether you will disconnect or hard-segment any internet-reachable Rockwell / Allen-Bradley environment. If you do nothing, you are accepting a live disruption risk — not a theoretical one.

Decision 2 — Trusted Access

If you rely on vendors, SaaS, MSPs, telecoms, or identity platforms, decide whether those relationships are verified strongly enough to survive compromise. The most likely business-impact event is now a trusted-path failure, not a noisy perimeter breach.

Decision 3 — Business Continuity

If one critical dependency failed tonight, decide whether your leadership team knows which operations break first, how fast cash flow is affected, and who owns the response. If you cannot answer that in one page, you are underprepared for this environment.

Texas is Facing a Multi-Actor Threat Environment, Not a Single Campaign

The most important insight this week is not any one adversary. It is the convergence of multiple capable actors applying pressure at the same time, through different access vectors, for different strategic purposes. Iran is demonstrating a willingness to generate real-world operational effects against OT systems. China continues to maintain quiet, durable access inside U.S. critical infrastructure while avoiding premature activation. North Korea is proving it can generate strategic-scale revenue through high-speed financial theft. Meanwhile, ransomware groups are exploiting the same gaps in identity, patching, and vendor trust that state actors use for access.

For Texas, this matters more than it does in most states because the state combines ERCOT, Gulf Coast petrochemicals, the Texas Medical Center, major financial hubs, and one of the densest defense and cyber ecosystems in the country. A successful operation against one sector is no longer a contained event. It can become a cascading business and public-safety problem within hours.

Bottom line: executives should stop asking whether they are likely to be targeted. The more urgent question is whether they would recognize compromise early enough to preserve decision space once disruption begins.

Section II

What Happened This Week

Iran — OT Disruption Has Crossed from Warning to Confirmed Effect

A rare joint advisory from FBI, CISA, NSA, EPA, DOE, and U.S. Cyber Command confirmed that Iranian-affiliated actors have been exploiting internet-exposed Rockwell Automation and Allen-Bradley PLC environments to generate real-world disruption across U.S. critical infrastructure. That matters because it signals intent to move beyond access and reconnaissance toward direct operational effects. For Texas, where OT density is unusually high across utilities, petrochemicals, and water systems, this is the week’s most important development.

Liberty CTI assesses that any Texas operator still treating exposed PLCs as a routine hardening issue is misreading the environment. For Allen-Bradley-heavy environments, this is now an active threat condition, not a planning scenario.

China — Pre-Positioning Remains the Core PRC Risk

PRC Typhoon operations continue to point to a doctrine of quiet access, infrastructure persistence, and crisis-contingency preparation. Volt Typhoon’s LOTL tradecraft remains significant precisely because it is difficult to detect with traditional malware-centric security stacks. Salt Typhoon’s telecom compromise reinforces a second-order risk that many executives still underestimate: communications themselves may be observable even when networks appear normal.

// Liberty CTI Assessment

The most dangerous PRC cyber outcome in the near term is not a visible outage. It is the persistence of unseen access that strips away warning time when geopolitical tension spikes.

Russia / Ransomware — Texas Remains in the Blast Radius of a Mature Criminal Ecosystem

Ransomware remains a top operational threat to Texas healthcare, government-adjacent, and manufacturing targets because the ecosystem now scales through supply-chain and identity compromise faster than many organizations patch or validate trust relationships. The broader lesson is that criminal operators are exploiting the same structural weaknesses that nation-state actors value: weak vendor governance, brittle identity controls, and uneven segmentation.

DPRK — Financial Theft Has Become a Strategic Capability

DPRK’s Lazarus ecosystem continues to treat cyber theft as state finance. The Drift Protocol theft underscored how little time defenders may have once access is established and approvals are manipulated. Texas-based financial institutions do not need to be crypto-native to care. The practical exposure runs through fintech partnerships, vendor integrations, software development pipelines, and any organization touching digital-asset infrastructure or adjacent services.

Section III

Geopolitical Drivers & Cyber Implications

Iran — Active Conflict Dynamics Are Increasing Cyber Risk to U.S. Infrastructure

What Happened: The current threat environment is being shaped by a broader escalation cycle involving Iran, regional proxy activity, maritime pressure, and direct friction with the United States and its partners.

Why It Matters: Iran uses cyber as an asymmetric instrument when it wants to impose costs, signal resolve, or retaliate without inviting an immediate conventional response. In that model, cyber is not secondary. It is one of the regime’s most practical tools for coercion.

Cyber Implication: The confirmed exploitation of Rockwell Automation and Allen-Bradley PLC environments is consistent with Iranian intent to move beyond reconnaissance and into disruptive effect. Liberty CTI assesses that Iran is using cyber to create pressure against U.S. critical infrastructure while preserving ambiguity and limiting escalation thresholds.

Business Risk (Texas Lens): For Texas, this raises direct concern for energy, water, petrochemical, and industrial operators whose OT environments are exposed, weakly segmented, or dependent on trusted remote access. Texas is not just economically relevant in this context. It is one of the most operationally meaningful target environments in the country.

// Executive Takeaway

Iran is the actor most likely to convert geopolitical tension into real-world disruption against Texas industrial systems.

China — Strategic Competition Is Driving Quiet Access, Not Immediate Disruption

What Happened: U.S.-China competition continues across semiconductors, AI, export controls, military posture in the Indo-Pacific, and telecommunications security. These are not isolated disputes. They are part of a larger strategic contest over technological and geopolitical advantage.

Why It Matters: Beijing’s cyber behavior reflects long-term planning. China does not need to cause visible disruption now to create strategic value. Persistent access inside infrastructure and telecom environments gives it decision advantage later, especially in a crisis tied to Taiwan or wider U.S.-China confrontation.

Cyber Implication: Volt Typhoon and related PRC operations should be understood as contingency preparation. Salt Typhoon’s telecom compromise reinforces a second, often underappreciated reality: China is not just targeting systems. It is targeting the information environment leaders rely on to make decisions.

Business Risk (Texas Lens): Texas sits at the intersection of energy infrastructure, AI buildout, defense concentration, and major telecommunications dependence. That makes the state highly relevant to PRC collection and pre-positioning logic, particularly where operational continuity, logistics, and communications overlap.

// Executive Takeaway

The greatest PRC risk in Texas is not a sudden outage tomorrow. It is that quiet access today reduces your warning time in a future crisis.

Russia — Protracted Conflict Continues to Fuel Spillover and Criminal Pressure

What Happened: Russia’s war in Ukraine remains a long-duration geopolitical driver shaping cyber behavior across both state and criminal ecosystems. Sanctions, confrontation with the West, and sustained wartime pressure continue to reinforce Moscow’s reliance on asymmetric tools.

Why It Matters: Russia benefits from a permissive criminal ecosystem that can generate disruption, revenue, and strategic friction below the threshold of formal state attribution. Even when attacks are financially motivated, the broader environment still serves Russian interests by imposing cost and uncertainty on Western institutions.

Cyber Implication: Ransomware and criminal compromise should not be treated as disconnected from the geopolitical environment. Texas organizations remain exposed to a mature Russia-based ecosystem that can scale through supply chain, credential abuse, and identity compromise faster than many enterprises can respond.

Business Risk (Texas Lens): Healthcare, manufacturing, municipal, and government-adjacent entities in Texas remain exposed to high-volume operational disruption, particularly where vendor concentration and weak identity assurance create shared pathways across multiple organizations.

// Executive Takeaway

Russia does not need a Texas-specific campaign to create Texas-specific damage. Spillover, tolerated criminal activity, and indirect pressure are enough.

DPRK — Regime Finance Requirements Are Sustaining High-Tempo Cyber Theft

What Happened: North Korea remains under heavy sanctions pressure and continues to rely on illicit finance to support regime survival, weapons development, and broader state priorities.

Why It Matters: For DPRK, cyber theft is not opportunistic side activity. It is a strategic revenue model. That means financial cyber operations will remain persistent, adaptive, and disciplined so long as they continue producing returns.

Cyber Implication: The Drift Protocol theft fits a long-standing North Korean pattern: patient access, rapid execution, and monetization at scale. Liberty CTI assesses that DPRK actors will continue prioritizing financial and crypto-adjacent pathways, including third-party platforms, software ecosystems, and workforce infiltration opportunities.

Business Risk (Texas Lens): Texas financial institutions, fintechs, crypto-adjacent firms, and software-enabled financial service providers are exposed less through headline bank compromise and more through integrations, vendors, developer environments, and trusted service relationships.

// Executive Takeaway

If your business touches money, digital assets, payment infrastructure, or remote technical labor, DPRK activity is part of your threat model.

Section IV

Texas Sector Implications

⚡ Energy
SEVERE
Primary risk: Iranian OT disruption layered over PRC pre-positioning.

Why Texas is exposed: ERCOT’s isolation, Gulf Coast industrial density, LNG export infrastructure, and heavy Rockwell / Allen-Bradley footprint make Texas one of the most operationally consequential energy target sets in the country.

Executive implication: Treat exposed OT, weak IT/OT segmentation, and incomplete anomaly detection as active business continuity vulnerabilities — not just security debt. Killer line: Texas grid isolation means there is no easy outside recovery path if a serious disruption lands here.
♥ Healthcare
SEVERE
Primary risk: Ransomware and destructive misuse of legitimate IT management tooling.

Why Texas is exposed: The Texas Medical Center and regional systems combine high operational pressure, sensitive data, shared vendors, and service continuity requirements that favor extortion actors.

Executive implication: A single vendor or identity compromise can become a clinical operations problem quickly. Recovery readiness matters as much as prevention. Killer line: In healthcare, cyber risk now threatens care timelines, not just data.
💰 Finance
HIGH
Primary risk: DPRK theft through integrations, vendors, and digitally enabled financial pathways.

Why Texas is exposed: Texas combines large banking, insurance, and fintech ecosystems with growing digital-asset adjacency and third-party dependency.

Executive implication: The highest-value question is no longer only “can our bank be hacked?” It is “which partner or platform could become the access vector into us?” Killer line: Third-party risk is now the front door to financial disruption.
🛡 Defense / DIB
HIGH
Primary risk: PRC espionage, telecom compromise, and identity / workforce infiltration.

Why Texas is exposed: JBSA, Port San Antonio, and the broader DIB ecosystem create a dense environment of cleared personnel, contractors, telecom dependence, and sensitive technical data.

Executive implication: Communications security, vendor assurance, and workforce validation are strategic controls here — not merely compliance exercises. Killer line: A telecom compromise can become a defense problem long before anyone sees malware.
⬡ AI / Data Centers
ELEVATED
Primary risk: Cascading exposure through power dependency, cloud concentration, and developer-focused intrusion pathways.

Why Texas is exposed: Texas’s AI and data-center buildout increases energy dependence and creates high-value digital concentration points that can amplify disruption elsewhere.

Executive implication: AI infrastructure should be treated as a cross-sector dependency, not a standalone technology niche. Killer line: Texas data-center growth is creating concentration risk faster than most companies are measuring it.

  • Unexplained OT access attempts or authentication events since March 2026

PRC access persistence is assessed as a strategic warning problem. The core issue is whether defenders are looking for the right artifacts.

  • Unusual use of native admin tools such as WMI, PowerShell, and netsh
  • Edge device configuration changes without approved change control
  • Low-volume outbound administrative traffic during non-business hours
  • Communications anomalies across major telecom-dependent executive workflows

  • Suspicious developer recruitment or contractor sourcing behavior
  • Third-party access requests into sensitive financial workflows
  • Vendor-side compromise with potential access to transaction or identity systems

  • Shared SaaS, MSP, or healthcare IT vendor incident notifications
  • Credential abuse against privileged or remote administrative accounts
  • Rapid exploitation following newly public proof-of-concept releases
  • Multiple Texas organizations impacted by a single upstream provider event

Section VII

Executive Implications & Recommended Actions

1 — Reevaluate Exposure to OT and Remote Access

If you run industrial or utility-adjacent operations, confirm what is internet-reachable, what can be remotely administered, and what would fail if trust in those systems collapsed. What happens if you get this wrong: downtime, safety pressure, and urgent leadership decisions under degraded visibility.

2 — Validate Your Top Five Dependencies

Map the vendors, telecom paths, identity platforms, cloud services, and payment or billing partners that keep operations running. What happens if you get this wrong: you discover your real risk through another company's outage instead of your own planning.

3 — Shift Detection Toward Trust and Identity

Prioritize privileged access, vendor sessions, MDM tooling, telecom exposure, and unusual use of native admin tools. What happens if you get this wrong: a low-noise compromise survives long enough to become a board-level continuity issue.

4 — Budget for Less Federal Cushion

Assume more of your early warning and response burden will sit with your organization and state-level relationships. What happens if you get this wrong: slower detection, slower coordination, and higher business impact when incidents unfold.

Section VIII

Analyst's Note

What matters most this week is the loss of sequencing. Texas organizations once had the luxury of looking at Iran, China, DPRK, and ransomware actors as mostly separate problems. That is no longer a useful frame. Iran is applying pressure at the OT layer. China is shaping the strategic environment through persistence and communications compromise. DPRK is extracting capital from the financial layer. Criminal actors are exploiting the same trust relationships all of them rely on.

This convergence changes the operating logic for leaders. It means that resilience must be cross-sector, not just technical. It means boards need to understand dependency mapping, not only cyber maturity scores. It means executive communications are part of the threat surface. And it means that a Texas company can be “not directly targeted” and still be operationally affected through a partner, a utility dependency, or a telecom compromise.

The practical question for leadership is simple: if one trusted system failed tonight — a utility connection, a hospital vendor, a telecom carrier, an identity platform, or a financial integration — how much time would we have before the problem became a business continuity decision? Most organizations still do not know that answer with enough precision.
